An interesting way of looking at the Cauchy-Riemann equations

This is a very short article pointing out a nice interpretation of the Cauchy-Riemann differential equations.

In the following, let \(p : \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{R}^2\) represent the canonical isomorphism between the real vector spaces \(\mathbb{C}\) and \(\mathbb{R}^2\).

Let \(u, v : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}\). The function \(u \circ p + (v \circ p)i\) is complex differentiable in \(z \in \mathbb{C}\) if and only if the partial derivatives of \(u\) and \(v\) exist in \(p(z)\) and the following equations hold:

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(p(z)) &= \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}(p(z)) \\ \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}(p(z)) &= -\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}(p(z)) \end{aligned}\]

While this theorem might seem surprising at first, it quickly turns trivial once you realize that the above equations are equivalent to saying that

\[ \nabla (p \circ f \circ p^{-1}) \in \left\langle \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\right\rangle \cong \mathbb{C}. \]
