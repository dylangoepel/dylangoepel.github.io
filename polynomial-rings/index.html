A brief introduction to polynomials

Definition

Let \(R\) be a unitary commutative ring. Define

\[ R[x] := \{ (a_n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \mid \exists n \in \mathbb{N}: \forall m \geq n: a_m = 0 \} \]

and \(+_{R[x]}: R[x] \times R[x] \to R[x]\), \(\cdot_{R[x]}: R[x] \times R[x] \to R[x]\), \(\phi_{R[x]}: R \to R[x]\) by

\[\begin{aligned} (a_n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}} +_{R[x]} (b_n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}} &:= (a_n + b_n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \\ (a_n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \cdot_{R[x]} (b_n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}} &:= \left(\sum_{i = 0}^n a_i b_{n - i}\right)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}. \\ \phi_{R[x]}(r) &:= \left( \begin{cases} r & n = 0 \\ 0 & n \not= 0 \end{cases} \right)_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \end{aligned}\]

Finally, define \(x \in R[x]\) by

\[ x_n := \begin{cases} 1 & n = 1 \\ 0 & n \not= 1. \end{cases} \]

It is easy to see that \((R[x], +_{R[x]}, \cdot_{R[x]}, \phi_{R[x]}(1))\) is a commutative unitary ring and that \(\phi_{R[x]}\) is a ring monomorphism. From now on, we will simply write \(r\) instead of \(\phi_{R[x]}(r)\) and \(+\), \(\cdot\) instead of \(+_{R[x]}\), \(\cdot_{R[x]}\).

Degree of a polynomial

Let \(p \in R[x]\). Define

\[\begin{aligned} \deg(p) = \max \{ n \in \mathbb{N} \mid p_n \not= 0 \} \cup \{-\infty\}. \end{aligned}\]

Representation of polynomials

Let \(p \in R[x]\). Define

\[\begin{aligned} q := \sum_{i = 0}^{\deg(p)} p_i x^i \in R[x]. \end{aligned}\]

First, notice that

\[\begin{aligned} (x^i)_n &= \begin{cases} 1 & n = i \\ 0 & n \not= i \end{cases} \end{aligned}\]

For \(n \in \mathbb{N}_0\), we have

\[\begin{aligned} q_n &= \sum_{i = 0}^{\deg(p)} (p_i x^i)_n \\\ &= \sum_{i = 0}^{\deg(p)} (p_i x^i)_n \\ &= \begin{cases} p_n & n \leq \deg(p) \\ 0 & n > \deg(p) \end{cases} \\ &= p_n \end{aligned}\]

and therefore

\[\begin{aligned} p &= \sum_{i = 0}^{\deg(p)} p_i x^i. \end{aligned}\]

Substitution homomorphisms

Let \(S\) be a ring and let \(\phi: R \to S\) be a ring homomorphism such that

\[ \forall s \in S: \forall r \in R:\quad s \cdot \phi(r) = \phi(r) \cdot s. \]

Let \(s \in S\). Define \(\phi_s : R[x] \to S\) by

\[ \phi_s\left( \sum_{i = 0}^{n} p_i x^i \right) := \sum_{i = 0}^{n} \phi(p_i) s^i. \]

It is easy to see that \(\phi_s\) is a ring homomorphism. From now on, we will call it the substitution homomorphism for \(s \in S\) over \(\phi\). If the underlying ring homomorphism is implicit, we will write \(p(s)\) instead of \(\phi_s(p)\).

If not explicitly specified, the underlying homomorphism will always be one of the following:

  • for \(n \in \mathbb{N}\): \(R \to R^{n \times n},\ r \mapsto r \cdot I_n\)

  • for a vector space \(V\) over \(R\): \(R \to \operatorname{Hom}(V, V),\ r \mapsto r \cdot \operatorname{id}_V\)

  • the identity isomorphism: \(R \to R\), \(r \mapsto r\)

Degree of polynomial products

Suppose \(R\) is an integral domain. Let \(p, q \in R[x]\). We have

\[\begin{aligned} (p \cdot q)_{\deg(p) + \deg(q)} &= \sum_{i = 0}^{\deg(p)+\deg(q)} p_i q_{\deg(p)+\deg(q)-i} \\ &= \underbrace{p_{\deg(p)}}_{\not=0} \underbrace{q_{\deg(q)}}_{\not= 0} \\ &\not= 0 \end{aligned}\]

and for \(n > \deg(p) + \deg(q)\)

\[\begin{aligned} (p \cdot q)_{n} &= \sum_{i = 0}^{n} p_i q_{n - i} \\ &= \sum_{i = 0}^{\deg(p)} p_i q_{n - i} \\ &= \sum_{i = n - \deg(q)}^{\deg(p)} p_i q_{n - i} \\\ &= 0 \end{aligned}\]

and therefore

\[\begin{aligned} \deg(p \cdot q) = \deg(p) + \deg(q). \end{aligned}\]

In particular, this implies that if \(R\) is an integral domain, \(R[x]\) is also an integral domain.
